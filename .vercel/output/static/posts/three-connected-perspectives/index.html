# Three Connected Perspectives

This article was originally intended to be about a notion of connectedness chain-connectedness which I find especially intuitive and visually appealing. In the process of writing, I realized that in practice, chain-connectedness isnt always the most efficient or appealing proof method. Often, it is easier to prove that some property holds locally, in a neighborhood of each point, than to prove it holds globally, for an entire space. Chain-connectedness offers a visual interpretation for how such local properties are patched together and extended to global ones, but it does not explicitly characterize which local properties can be extended to global ones.

In what follows, I describe my three favorite characterizations of connectedness and explain how they are related. The first frames connectedness as a type of structural induction, the explanation of which I delegate to another blog; the second is chain-connectedness, a discretization of path-connectedness; the final characterization makes precise which local properties can be extended to global ones and frames connectedness as the principle that any distinction of points which vanishes locally must vanish globally.

note

The empty set vacuously satisfies all of the equivalent characterizations of connectedness that follow. Regardless of your personal convention whether or not the empty set is connected we can assume, without loss of generality, that all spaces XXX are non-empty from this point on.

Connectedness as an Induction Principle

Our initial characterization of connectedness will be as follows:

Definition (Connected)

A topological space XXX is said to be disconnected provided that there exists two non-empty, disjoint, open subsets of XXX which union to XXX. The space XXX is said to be connected provided that it is not disconnected.

Intuitively, this notion of connectedness says that the non-empty open subsets of XXX cannot separate XXX.

Over at Pseudoniums Blog, connectedness as an induction principle is motivated by and generalizes real induction. Roughly, the induction principle characterizes a connected space by its clopen subsets, of which there can only be two the whole space and the empty space.

Theorem (Induction Principle for Connectedness)

A topological space XXX is connected if and only if for every subset SSS of XXX such that

  • SSS is non-empty
  • SSS is open
  • SSS is closed

then S=XS = XS=X.

Proof

Suppose that XXX is connected. Let SSS be a subset of XXX satisfying the three properties above, i.e., SSS is a non-empty clopen subset. If SSS is a proper subset of XXX, then XSX - SXS is also a non-empty, open subset of XXX; XXX is thus the union of two non-empty, disjoint, open subsets, a contradiction.

Conversely, suppose XXX is a space satisfying the inductive principle that the only non-empty clopen subset of XXX is XXX. Let UUU and VVV be two non-empty, disjoint, open subsets which union to XXX. The set U=XVU = X - VU=XV is a non-empty, clopen subset of XXX. By the inductive principle on XXX, U=XU = XU=X, contradicting the fact that VVV is non-empty. Since XXX cannot be disconnected, XXX is connected.

This characterization describes necessary and sufficient conditions that a subset of a connected space must satisfy to be the entire space. If a subset SSS of a connected space XXX is defined by a predicate \phi on XXX as S={xX:(x)}S = \{x \in X : \phi(x)\}S={xX:(x)}, the induction principle for connectedness states that \phi is true at every point of XXX provided that

  • (x)\phi(x)(x) is true at some point xSx \in SxS,
  • on SSS, \phi is locally true, meaning that for each inhabitant xSx \in SxS, there is an open set UUU such that xUSx \in U \subseteq SxUS and (u)\phi(u)(u) is true for all uUu \in UuU, and
  • on XSX - SXS, \phi is locally false.

We have met the first of my three favorite perspectives on connectedness.

It is hard not to mention that this last observation leads naturally to the characterization of connectedness by continuous functions into discrete spaces; however, this isnt one of the three perspectives I will be discussing.

Related is the notion of compactness as an induction principle.

Chain-Connectedness

After learning about chain-connectedness during my undergrad (my original favorite characterization), I found it to be extremely intuitive, easy to visualize, and made certain proofs more transparent. Additionally, like its topological counterpart compactness chain-connectedness is a characterization of connectedness in terms of open covers.

Definitions

The preliminary notion of a chain is useful for defining chain-connectedness:

Definition (Chain and Chain-Connected Open Covers)

Let XXX be a topological space and U\mathcal{U}U be an open cover of XXX (a collection of open subsets of XXX which union to XXX).

Given two open subsets U,VUU,V \in \mathcal{U}U,VU, a chain from UUU to VVV in U\mathcal{U}U is a finite sequnece {U0,,Un}\{U_0,\dots,U_{n}\}{U0,,Un} in U\mathcal{U}U such that

  • U0=UU_0 = UU0=U,
  • Un=VU_n = VUn=V, and
  • UiUi+1U_{i} \cap U_{i + 1} \neq \emptysetUiUi+1= for each 0i<n0 \leq i < n0i<n.

For xUx \in UxU and yVy \in VyV, the sequence {U0,,Un}\{U_{0},\dots,U_{n}\}{U0,,Un} is called a chain from xxx to yyy in U\mathcal{U}U.

The open cover U\mathcal{U}U is a chain-connected open cover if there is a chain between any two non-empty open sets in U\mathcal{U}U, or equivalently, between any two points of XXX.

As the name suggests, the open sets of a chain (in an open cover) form the chain links, and they are linked together by having non-trivial overlap. Just like jewelry, chains can loop back on themselves and have repeated links.

Definition (Chain-Connected)

A topological space XXX is said to be chain-connected if every open cover of XXX is a chain-connected open cover of XXX.

Explicitly, XXX is chain-connected if for every open cover U\mathcal{U}U of XXX and any two non-empty open sets U,VUU,V \in \mathcal{U}U,VU, there is a finite sequence {U0,,Un}\{U_{0},\dots,U_{n}\}{U0,,Un} in U\mathcal{U}U such that

  • U0=UU_{0} = UU0=U,
  • Un=VU_{n} = VUn=V, and
  • UiUi+1U_{i} \cap U_{i + 1} \neq \emptysetUiUi+1= for each 0i<n0 \leq i < n0i<n.

Before moving on, lets contrast this with the notion of path-connectedness:

Definition (Path and Path-Connected)

A path in a topological space XXX is a continuous function :IX\gamma : I \to X:IX, where I=[0,1]I = [0,1]I=[0,1] is the unit interval (with the usual topology).

The space XXX is said to be path-connected provided that there is a path between any two points of XXX.

The notion of chain-connectedness may be seen as a discretization of path-connectedness; instead of any two points being joined by a path of points in XXX, roughly, any two open sets (or equivalently, points) are joined by a path of open sets in XXX. This observation suggests a natural visualization of chains and chain-connectedness.

The main reason I like chain-connectedness so much is because of its visual and geometric nature which I feel other characterizations lack; it becomes more intuitive after trying to apply it, as we will now.

The Link to Connectedness

It is a familiar theorem that every path-connected space is connected. The discrete analogue of this is also true; every chain-connected space is connected. As suggested, these notions of connectedness are also equivalent.

Theorem (Chain-Connectedness and Connectedness are Equivalent)

A topological space XXX is connected if and only it is chain-connected.

Proof

Apply the induction principle for connectedness!

Let U\mathcal{U}U be an open cover of XXX. Declare two points xxx and yyy of XXX to be equivalent if and only if there is a chain in U\mathcal{U}U from xxx to yyy and write xyx \sim yxy; indeed, this defines an equivalence relation on XXX.

Say that x0x_0x0 is a point of XXX; define S={xX:xx0}S = \{x \in X : x \sim x_0\}S={xX:xx0}.

  • SSS is non-empty: x0Sx_0 \in Sx0S.
  • SSS is open: for xSx \in SxS, there is a chain {U0,,Un}\{U_0,\dots,U_n\}{U0,,Un} in U\mathcal{U}U from x0x_0x0 to xxx. For each yUny \in U_nyUn, the same chain is a chain from x0x_0x0 to yyy, thus UnU_nUn is an open neighborhood of xxx contained in SSS.
  • SSS is closed: for xXSx \in X - SxXS, there is an open set UUU \in \mathcal{U}UU containing xxx. UUU cannot intersect SSS, since otherwise there would be a chain from x0x_0x0 to xxx.

By the inductive principle for connectedness, S=XS = XS=X, thus XXX is chain-connected.

Conversely, suppose XXX is chain-connected. Let SSS be a non-empty clopen subset of XXX. The set U={S,XS}\mathcal{U} = \{S, X - S\}U={S,XS} is an open cover of XXX, so there is a chain in U\mathcal{U}U between any two of its non-empty open subsets. No finite sequence in U\mathcal{U}U can form a chain from SSS to XSX - SXS; therefore XSX - SXS must be empty, i.e., S=XS = XS=X. Having demonstrated that XXX satisfies the induction principle for connectedness, XXX is connected.

The equivalence relation above will be referred to as the equivalence relation determined by chains.

Having shown that these two notions of connectedness are equivalent, we have met the second of my three favorite perspectives on connectedness.

Connectedness as a Local-to-Global Principle

Any subset SSS of a set XXX induces an equivalence relation xyx\sim yxy if and only if x,ySx,y \in Sx,yS or x,yXSx,y \in X - Sx,yXS. If XXX is a topological space, and SSS is clopen, then the equivalence classes of \sim, SSS and XSX - SXS, are both open. If we think of equivalence relations as a way to distinguish points of a space, the distinction of points of XXX by belonging to SSS or not belonging to SSS vanishes locally. In a connected space, that is enough for the distinction to vanish globally; therefore, if XXX is connected, and SSS is non-empty, then S=XS = XS=X.

This observation leads us to my final favorite perspective on connectedness. At least one other source calls this the local-to-global lemma. First, it is useful to make precise what is meant by a distinction vanishing locally and globally.

Definition ((Locally) Trivial Equivalence Relation)

An equivalence relation \sim on a set XXX is trivial if =XX\sim \, = X \times X=XX, that is, if every element of XXX is related to every other element of XXX, or, if \sim has a single equivalence class.

If \sim is a relation on a topological space XXX, \sim is called locally trivial if there is an open neighborhood UUU around each point of XXX on which xyx \sim yxy for all x,yUx,y \in Ux,yU (equivalently, if the equivalence classes of \sim are open).

Locally trivial equivalence relations are ways to distinguish of points of a space where the distinction vanishes locally, i.e., each point is contained in an open neighborhood where all points are equal.

Theorem (The Local-to-Global Lemma)

A topological space XXX is connected if and only if every locally trivial equivalence relation on XXX is (globally) trivial.

Proof

Suppose XXX is connected. Let \sim be a locally trivial equivalence relation on XXX. Each equivalence class of \sim is non-empty and clopen. By the induction principle for connectedness, each equivalence class is equal to XXX. The equivalence relation \sim has a unique equivalence class and is therefore trivial.

Conversely, let SSS be a non-empty clopen subset of XXX. The equivalence relation xyx \sim yxy if and only if x,ySx,y \in Sx,yS or x,yXSx,y \in X - Sx,yXS is a locally trivial equivalence relation, and is thus promoted to a globally trivial one. Let x0Sx_0 \in Sx0S. Every element xXx \in XxX is related to x0x_0x0, and so S=XS = XS=X. By the induction principle for connectedness, XXX is connected.

The local-to-global lemma frames connectedness as the principle that any distinction which vanishes locally must vanish globally. It also says that the local properties which can be extended to global ones are exactly those which induce a locally trivial equivalence relation.

It is interesting to specialize the above proof of the converse direction to the case when SSS is defined by a predicate on XXX. It is also interesting to specialize the above proof of the forwards direction when \sim is the equivalence relation determined by chains.

Applications

Here are a few applications of my favorite notions of connectedness.

Proposition

If XXX is connected and locally path-connected, then XXX is path-connected.

Proof

Path-connectedness is an equivalence relation on any space; being locally path-connected means that it is a locally trivial equivalence relation (locally, any two points are equivalent). By the local-to-global lemma, XXX is path-connected.

Proposition

Every locally constant function on a connected space XXX is constant.

Proof

Let f:XYf : X \to Yf:XY be a locally constant function on a connected space XXX.

Say that xyx \sim yxy if and only if f(x)=f(y)f(x) = f(y)f(x)=f(y). Any distinctions among points with respect to \sim vanishes locally (fff is locally constant); by the local-to-global lemma, fff is constant.

Proposition

The product of finitely many connected spaces is connected.

Proof

By induction, it suffices to show that the product of two connected spaces is connected. We use chain-connectedness.

Let XXX and YYY be connected and U\mathcal{U}U an open cover of XYX \times YXY. The projection X:XYX\pi_X : X \times Y \to XX:XYX is an open map, and so X(U)={X(U):UU}\pi_X(\mathcal{U}) = \{\pi_X(U) : U \in \mathcal{U}\}X(U)={X(U):UU} is an open cover of XXX, likewise for YYY.

Given (a,b),(c,d)XY(a,b),(c,d) \in X \times Y(a,b),(c,d)XY, there is a finite chain {X(U0),,X(Un)}\{\pi_X(U_0),\dots,\pi_X(U_n)\}{X(U0),,X(Un)} in X(U)\pi_X(\mathcal{U})X(U) from aaa to ccc. Likewise, there is a finite chain {Y(V0),,Y(Vm)}\{\pi_Y(V_0),\dots,\pi_Y(V_m)\}{Y(V0),,Y(Vm)} in Y(U)\pi_Y(\mathcal{U})Y(U) from bbb to ddd. The set {U0,,Un,V0,,Vm}\{U_0,\dots,U_n,V_0,\dots,V_m\}{U0,,Un,V0,,Vm} is a chain in U\mathcal{U}U from (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) to (c,d)(c,d)(c,d) since (c,b)UnV0(c,b) \in U_n \cap V_0(c,b)UnV0.

Proposition

If AAA is a connected subspace of XXX, if ABclAA \subseteq B \subseteq \mathtt{cl}\,AABclA, then BBB is connected.

Proof

We use chain-connectedness.

Let U\mathcal{U}U be an open cover of BBB and fix x,yBx,y \in Bx,yB. There are four cases:

  1. (xA,yA)(x \in A, y \in A)(xA,yA) There is a chain in U\mathcal{U}U from xxx to yyy since AAA is connected.

  2. (xA,yBA)(x \in A, y \in B - A)(xA,yBA) In this case, yyy is a limit point of AAA; there is some neighborhood UUU \in \mathcal{U}UU of yyy and some zU(A{y})z \in U \cap (A - \{y\})zU(A{y}). The first two propositions above can also be proven using chain-connectedness, which shows more explicitly how to patch together the local data into global data.

This next batch of applications uses a bit more machinery, so I will only give proof sketches.

Here is an adaptation of Problem 4-31 from Lees Introduction to Topological Manifolds.

Proposition

Let XXX be locally Euclidean Hausdorff of dimension nnn and paracompact.

If XXX has countably many connected components, then XXX is a topological nnn-manifold, that is, a second countable locally Euclidean Hausdorff space.

Proof

It suffices to assume that XXX is connected such a space is locally connected, so its connected components are open. A countable basis on each connected component of XXX therefore assembles into a countable basis on the entire space.

Let V\mathcal{V}V be the collection of all open subsets of XXX which are nnn-manifolds and whose closure is compact; V\mathcal{V}V is non-empty and covers XXX since each point is contained in such a neighborhood. By paracompactness, there is a locally finite refinement U\mathcal{U}U of V\mathcal{V}V whose elements are all non-empty.

Fix U0UU_0 \in \mathcal{U}U0U; by (chain-)connectedness, there is a finite chain in U\mathcal{U}U from U0U_0U0 to UUU for UUU \in \mathcal{U}UU. Let f:UNf : \mathcal{U} \to \mathbb{N}f:UN be the function defined by taking each UUU \in \mathcal{U}UU to the length of a shortest chain from U0U_0U0 to UUU in U\mathcal{U}U. By local-finiteness, the fibers of fff are finite, thus U\mathcal{U}U is countable.

Any locally Euclidean Hausdorff space of dimension nnn which can be covered by countably many compact sets is second countable (see Problem 4-16 or this paper for details).

For each point ppp of the open unit ball Bn\mathbb{B}^nBn of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, there is a smooth diffeomorphism of Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn which is the identity outside of Bn\mathbb{B}^nBn and takes the origin to ppp. Such a map can be chosen to be smoothly isotopic to the identity a description can be found in Milnors Topology from a Differentiable Viewpoint on pages 23 and 24.

By solving the problem locally, we obtain the following homogeneity result:

Proposition

Let MMM be a connected manifold (topological or differentiable of any order). The homeomorphism/diffeomorphism group of MMM acts transitively on MMM. Moreover, such a homeomorphism/diffeomorphism can be chosen to be (smoothly) isotopic to the identity.

Proof

Say two points x,yx,yx,y of MMM are equivalent if there is homeomorphism/diffeomorphism taking xxx to yyy. This determines a locally trivial equivalence relation. By the local-to-global lemma, any two points of MMM are related by a homeomorphism/diffeomorphism.
